The Misconception Behind Sharks

There are about 465 different recorded species of sharks. They come in all different shapes and sizes and have adapted to living in many different aquatic habitats. At any given time, anywhere in the world’s ocean, there is always the possibility of spotting a shark. This fact alone has terrified people for hundreds of years as sharks are considered by most to be very dangerous and scary creatures. Many people tend to think of them more as monsters than animals.

Why Is This?

There are many reasons that people see these creatures in such a monsterly manner. A massive contribution to this issue is ignorance. When people think of sharks they imagine the speed, strength, and teeth, but most people dont actually know much about these animals and their behaviors. Another massive contribution to this misconception is the way they are portryaed in cinema, paritcularly in horror movies like Jaws, 47 meters down, open water, etc. Sharks are apex predators that exist in a world completely out of our element. Movies like the ones mentioned use this fact to create an overwhelming sense of suspense, panic and fear around these creatures. Most people have subconsciously learned to fear sharks deeply, when in reality there is a better chance of getting hit by lightning than being attacked by one.

Who Cares?

People don't often consider the impact that such a misconception can have on different shark species all over the world. The problem isn't just that people see them as dangerous and scary, but the way they are treated as a result. Up to 100 million sharks are killed every year by human beings. That's about 11,000 every hour. This is mainly done for their fins which are used to create shark fin soup, a delicacy in many places, particularly Asian countries. It is also done in order to make certain areas safer for swimming. Shark populations are dropping rapidly and the severity of this issue needs to be understood. Sharks are at the top of their food chain and for this reason they play a crucial role in regulating the ecosystems they belong to. They not only control population sizes, but are prone to hunting old, weak or sick prey, a behavior which improves different species via natural selection. If shark populations continue to drop at this rate hundreds if not thousands of aquatic species could be lost, creating devastating effects on the ocean’s ecosystems.
